About This Space

Approach

This space examines diverse traditions of movement and awareness - from contemporary movement culture to traditional practices like yoga, alongside somatic approaches like Feldenkrais and Authentic Movement. It's where I investigate how these different paths can open up possibilities for freedom in both movement and being.

Drawing insights from both physical practices and contemplative traditions, I'm discovering how movement and awareness can gradually expand our capacity for authentic expression by gently pushing back the boundaries that fear and protective instincts have drawn.

The Natural Tension

Natural Instincts

We're born with instincts for movement, expression, and connection. These natural impulses draw us toward growth and authenticity, showing us who we could be.

Learned Protection

In a world with real consequences, we learn to protect ourselves. Fear of judgement, hurt, or isolation teaches us to constrict - to make ourselves smaller in both body and feeling.

Understanding Restriction

When fear leads us to contain our natural expression, its influence reaches deep:

In Our Body

We learn to hold back physical impulses through tension and restricted movement. What begins as protection becomes a habit of holding ourselves in check, gradually diminishing our natural capacity for movement and expression.

In Our Emotions

Our mind develops ways to shield us from feelings that seem unsafe - it may tell us stories to avoid vulnerability, uncertainty, or being overwhelmed. It shapes how we see reality to keep our emotional world smaller, limiting our ability to fully experience and process our feelings.

In Our Relationships

The ways we protect ourselves shape our relationships in ways we might not even see, sometimes recreating the very patterns we were trying to avoid.

Finding Freedom

My exploration centers on these parallel paths. Throughout history and across cultures - from contemporary movement culture to yoga, martial arts, traditional dance forms, and meditative movement practices like qigong and Sufi whirling - people have discovered how mindful movement both expands physical freedom and helps rediscover natural playful expression. Similarly, but through a different path, depth-oriented psychotherapy offers its own journey of transformation, using consistent internal attention to gradually reshape our relationship with ourselves and create space for authentic expression.

Movement & Embodiment

  • Developing physical strength, mobility, and skill to support confident movement
  • Building awareness of internal sensations and natural impulses
  • Rediscovering joy and playfulness in expression
  • Creating a bridge back to parts we've kept small

Therapeutic Depth

  • Exploring and reshaping our internal world through reflection and connection
  • Learning new ways to hold our experience
  • Building emotional capacity as we meet what we've been avoiding
  • Freeing up energy previously used for protection

These approaches work together to create space for both safety and growth. As our inner world expands, we discover a natural freedom to move, feel, and connect in ways that express our authentic selves.

Movement & Emotion: The Butterfly Transformation

Drawing from both contemporary psychological research and ancient wisdom traditions, this framework explores how we can work with different levels of emotional experience. While Lecours & Bouchard's theory provides a theoretical foundation for understanding how we process experience, the butterfly metaphor offers a practical approach to exploring and integrating these different registers of experience in a natural, developmental way.

Registers of Experience

Drawing from Lecours & Bouchard's theory, experience can be processed at different levels:

  • Somatic Register: Raw physiological sensations and bodily experiences - the most basic level of feeling and experiencing
  • Motoric Register: Expression through movement and action - how we embody and act out our experiences
  • Imaginal Register: Mental images, metaphors, and visualisations - processing through internal pictures and scenarios
  • Verbal Register: Expression through language and words - the most abstract level of processing experience

The Butterfly Practice

A structured approach to exploring embodied experience through four stages:

  • Egg Stage: Opening to diffuse body awareness - moving away from our habitual avoidance of internal sensations. Unlike a sequential body scan, this stage cultivates a spacious, holistic attention to what's present. This stage may result in a deeper connection with the body and clearer recognition of internal sensations.
  • Caterpillar Stage: Discovering and following movement impulses that naturally arise from this internal awareness. These movements are explored gradually and safely, allowing authentic expression to emerge. This stage may result in a fuller understanding of how these sensations want to move through us.
  • Chrysalis Stage: Creating a stable container of alert stillness where we practice holding awareness of impulses without immediately acting on them. Cultivating this capacity creates space for dreamlike states of playfulness, curiosity, and creativity, where meaning and understanding can flourish in the space between impulse and action.
  • Butterfly Stage: Moving into paired sharing and deep listening, where participants take turns giving voice to their experience. This final stage offers the opportunity to discover words that contain the experience in more abstract, symbolic forms that can be explored and understood in new ways.

Understanding the Depths

While each stage of this practice unfolds naturally, the transformations it supports reflect deep patterns in how human beings process and integrate experience. Various fields - from neuroscience to contemplative traditions - have mapped different aspects of these patterns. Of particular interest is how this practice aligns with research into psychological development and with ancient wisdom about embodied transformation.

Theoretical and Traditional Perspectives

The progression through these stages reflects insights from multiple fields about how we process and transform experience. Lecours and Bouchard's model of mentalisation describes how raw experience becomes increasingly elaborated through different channels - somatic, motor, imagery, and verbal - and different levels of containment and processing. Their framework offers one systematic way to understand the transformations that can occur in this practice, while various research traditions and contemplative approaches illuminate additional aspects of how these transformations happen.

The Egg Stage: Somatic Foundations

In Lecours and Bouchard's model, all psychological experience begins in what they call the somatic register - our basic bodily sensations and internal states. They describe how this foundation of raw somatic experience needs to be accessed before it can be transformed through higher levels of processing. Their research reveals how early psychological development depends on this capacity to recognise and stay with basic bodily experience.

Contemporary neuroscience has revealed how this kind of internal awareness engages specific neural networks that support both emotional intelligence and physiological regulation. The vagus nerve, for instance, creates a bidirectional highway between body and brain, allowing this gentle attention to literally reshape our nervous system's patterns. Meanwhile, contemplative traditions have mapped this territory through careful observation - Buddhist mindfulness describes how sustained attention to body sensation naturally dissolves our usual conceptual frameworks, while Taoist traditions work with ever-subtler layers of internal sensation as gateways to deeper awareness.

The Caterpillar Stage: Movement as Bridge

Lecours and Bouchard identify the motor register as a crucial channel through which raw experience begins to find expression and form. Their model suggests that motor activity provides a natural bridge between pure sensation and more elaborated forms of representation. They describe different levels of motor expression, from impulsive discharge to more contained and purposeful movement.

The practice of Authentic Movement has discovered how following natural impulses allows movement to emerge from a place deeper than conscious control, revealing patterns of experience seeking completion. Contemporary trauma theory has found that the body often holds interrupted movement patterns - subtle impulses that, when allowed to complete, can release long-held tension patterns. In Sufi traditions, movement becomes a direct path to ecstatic states, while qigong works with subtle internal movements as keys to unlocking blocked energy.

The Chrysalis Stage: The Container

One of Lecours and Bouchard's key insights is about how experience becomes increasingly mentalised through different levels of containment. They describe a progression from raw discharge through modulated expression to more sophisticated forms of holding and processing. Their model reveals how creating a stable container allows new forms of representation to emerge.

Modern trauma theory describes how this kind of contained awareness helps expand our "window of tolerance" - the range of experience we can stay present with rather than moving into fight, flight, or freeze. Contemplative traditions speak of the profound space that opens up between stimulus and response when we learn to neither act on nor suppress what arises. This space allows what neuroscience calls vertical integration - the linking of body sensation, emotional experience, and cognitive understanding.

The Butterfly Stage: Integration Through Expression

The verbal register in Lecours and Bouchard's model represents the most sophisticated level of mental elaboration, where experience can be symbolised and shared. Importantly, they emphasise that this isn't just about putting words to experience, but about a fundamental transformation in how experience is held and understood.

Psychodynamic theory describes how deep listening creates a special kind of mental space called reverie - where by attending not just to words but to tone, gesture, and our own internal responses, while allowing space for silence, we can access and reflect back deeper layers of meaning. This kind of attention engages what neuroscience calls right-brain communication - beneath our conscious verbal exchanges, our emotional and embodied systems are in constant subtle dialogue. When both people attend to their internal experience while staying in relationship, a kind of resonance can emerge, like two tuning forks beginning to vibrate in harmony. This creates moments of being understood at levels deeper than words can easily reach.

This profound kind of listening appears in various traditions - from contemplative practices of bearing witness to indigenous councils' practices of deep listening. Each tradition has discovered how this quality of presence, combined with spaciousness and attention to subtle internal signals, can create containers where new kinds of understanding and connection become possible. The effect is not just cognitive but deeply emotional - moments of being truly seen and accepted at levels beneath our usual social interactions, which can transform how we relate to our own experience.

Together, these perspectives illuminate different aspects of how transformation happens through embodied awareness, movement, creative holding, and witnessed expression. Each field - whether neuroscience, contemplative traditions, somatic psychology, or psychodynamic understanding - has mapped different aspects of these natural processes of unfolding and integration.

The Butterfly Practice: A Path to Embodied Integration

The Butterfly Practice guides participants through a natural progression of how humans process and integrate experience. Drawing from Lecours and Bouchard's research on mentalization, the practice works systematically with four levels through which experience can be processed: somatic (body sensation), motoric (movement), imaginal (creative holding), and verbal (witnessed expression).

Their research reveals how psychological development follows this progression - from basic bodily experience through movement and imagination to verbal expression. Each level builds upon and requires the previous ones. We can't effectively verbalize what we haven't first sensed, moved with, and held in awareness. Similarly, we can't fully access movement possibilities without first building capacity for internal sensing.

Natural Progression

The practice's four stages - Egg, Caterpillar, Chrysalis, and Butterfly - mirror this natural progression, each building essential capacities that support the ones that follow:

  • The Egg stage works with the somatic register, developing our capacity to sense and stay with bodily experience
  • The Caterpillar stage engages the motoric register, allowing natural movement to emerge from our enhanced body awareness
  • The Chrysalis stage cultivates the imaginal register, developing our ability to hold experience in creative awareness
  • The Butterfly stage works with the verbal register, using witnessed expression to support integration and understanding

Multiple Perspectives

This progression aligns with insights from multiple fields:

  • Neuroscience shows how body awareness supports emotional regulation, how movement processes emotion, how pausing supports integration, and how social connection enables transformation
  • Somatic psychology reveals how working with these different levels helps access and integrate experience
  • Contemplative traditions map how awareness progressively refines through similar stages
  • Movement practices demonstrate how embodied exploration supports natural development

The practice creates a container where this integration can happen naturally. Rather than forcing change, it supports the conditions where transformation tends to emerge on its own. This follows nature's pattern - just as a caterpillar doesn't need to be taught how to become a butterfly, given the right conditions, our natural capacity for growth and integration can unfold.

The Egg Stage: Awakening Internal Awareness

The practice begins with developing our capacity for internal sensing through gentle, diffuse awareness of bodily experience. In Lecours and Bouchard's framework, this connects us with the somatic register - the foundation of all psychological experience. Their research reveals how accessing and staying with basic bodily experience is essential before it can be transformed through higher levels of processing.

The Science of Internal Awareness

Interoception: Our Internal Sensing System

Modern neuroscience has revealed that we have a dedicated sensing system for detecting internal bodily states - tension, temperature, pressure, and movement within our organs and tissues. This system, called interoception, creates detailed maps of our internal state that connect body sensations with emotional experience. Research shows that people with better interoceptive awareness tend to have greater emotional intelligence, make decisions that honor their needs, and experience a stronger sense of embodied self.

The Vagus Nerve: Our Body-Mind Integration Highway

The vagus nerve plays a crucial role in this internal awareness, creating a two-way communication channel between body and brain. About 80% of its fibers carry information upward from the body, connecting to major organs and helping regulate our physiological state. This extensive network explains why gentle attention to body sensations can have such profound effects on our wellbeing.

Polyvagal theory helps us understand how this kind of awareness practice supports transformation. When we practice gentle attention in a safe context, we engage our ventral vagal system - our newest evolutionary development for social engagement and safety. This creates physiological conditions that support exploration and integration of experience, gradually reshaping our default nervous system patterns toward greater flexibility and resilience.

Traditional Wisdom and Contemporary Applications

Ancient Practices

The Buddhist tradition of kāyānupassanā (mindfulness of the body) offers systematic practices for developing this internal awareness. They observe that sustained attention to physical sensation naturally quiets mental chatter, allowing direct experience to become clearer. As conceptual thinking subsides, we can observe how our usual mental frameworks often distort experience, making body awareness a more reliable anchor for understanding ourselves.

Taoist neigong practices map a progression of increasingly subtle internal awareness. Practitioners discover how initial attention reveals obvious sensations like tension and temperature, but with practice, subtler experiences like internal flows and pulses become noticeable. These subtler sensations provide valuable feedback about our energetic and emotional state, helping guide us toward more balanced ways of being.

Modern Applications

Contemporary therapeutic approaches build on these insights about the importance of bodily awareness. In trauma therapy, gentle somatic tracking helps rebuild connection with internal experience that may have been lost through overwhelming experiences. The Focusing approach, developed through research on successful therapy outcomes, shows how attending to unclear "felt senses" in the body allows new meanings to emerge. Internal Family Systems therapy demonstrates how different aspects of our experience have distinct bodily signatures that can help us understand and work with them more effectively.

Creating Conditions for Awareness

This stage creates space for natural attention to emerge rather than forcing concentration. Like waiting for eyes to adjust in a dimly lit room, we allow our internal awareness to gradually become clearer. This gentle approach engages our natural capacity for interoception while avoiding the tension that often comes with trying too hard to focus.

The effects of this practice tend to unfold in layers. Initially, we might notice obvious sensations - areas of tension, the movement of breath, temperature variations. As our sensing becomes more refined, we may discover subtler experiences - slight movements, emotional resonances, energy flows. This progressive refinement of awareness creates a foundation for the more active stages that follow.

From Sensing to Movement

As our capacity for internal sensing develops, we often begin to notice subtle impulses toward movement. These might start as barely perceptible desires to shift or adjust - a finger wanting to flex, a shoulder wanting to release, the spine wanting to elongate or curve. This natural progression from sensation to movement reflects what Lecours and Bouchard observed: that the motoric register emerges from and is supported by our somatic foundation.

The transition from pure sensing to movement isn't forced or rushed. Just as we allowed awareness to emerge naturally in the Egg stage, we now create space for movement to arise organically from this enhanced sensitivity. The stronger our connection with internal sensation, the clearer these movement impulses become. It's as if the body, feeling heard through our patient attention, begins to speak more directly through motion.

The Caterpillar Stage: Following Movement Impulses

Building on the foundation of internal awareness, this stage opens space for natural movement to emerge. In Lecours and Bouchard's framework, this engages the motoric register - a crucial channel through which raw somatic experience begins to find expression and form. Their research shows how motor activity provides a natural bridge between pure sensation and more elaborated forms of representation, ranging from impulsive discharge to increasingly contained and purposeful movement.

The Science of Movement Development

Movement Emergence

Movement science helps us understand how new possibilities for expression emerge. Dynamic systems theory shows that rather than being pre-programmed, our movements arise from the continuous interaction between our internal state, our body's capabilities, and our environment. This means that by creating different conditions - like feeling safe enough to explore or finding new ways to support our weight - we can discover movement possibilities that were always potentially available but had remained unexpressed.

This understanding aligns with research on motor learning and neural plasticity. When we explore movement with curiosity rather than fixed goals, we create rich sensorimotor maps in our brain. These maps become more detailed through varied experience, continuing to develop throughout life. This plasticity means we can develop new movement possibilities at any age, especially when we approach movement with playful exploration rather than rigid practice.

Movement and Emotion

The connection between movement and emotion runs deep in our nervous system. Research on mirror neurons reveals that the same neural circuits activate whether we perform an action or see others perform it. This system appears to help us understand others' emotions through their movements and feel what they feel through embodied empathy. The implication is profound - our movement and emotional experiences are intrinsically linked, each capable of influencing and expressing the other.

Traditional Movement Wisdom

Chinese Internal Arts

In Chinese internal arts, the practice of zifagong (spontaneous qigong) creates conditions where movement arises without conscious direction. Practitioners observe how these movements often feel meaningful or therapeutic, as if the body knows what it needs to release blocked energy or find better organization.

Sufi Movement Practice

The Mevlevi Sufi tradition of sama (whirling) demonstrates how sustained movement can shift our state of consciousness. Through the practice of spinning, practitioners move beyond normal conscious control into states of expanded awareness. This illustrates how movement can both express and transform our state of being, creating conditions where new kinds of experience become possible.

Contemporary Somatic Approaches

Body-Mind Centering

Body-Mind Centering maps how basic movement patterns emerge in sequence - from cellular breathing through navel radiation, spinal patterns, and increasingly complex coordinations. Each pattern creates foundation for richer movement possibilities while supporting specific aspects of psychological development. This suggests that returning to basic patterns can help reorganize both our movement and our way of being.

Authentic Movement

The practice of Authentic Movement provides a framework for exploring emergent movement. In the presence of a witness, movers close their eyes to heighten internal awareness and wait for movement impulses. This container allows them to discover how movement often carries implicit meaning, revealing aspects of experience that hadn't yet found expression. The presence of an accepting witness helps create safety for this exploration.

The Feldenkrais Method

The Feldenkrais Method demonstrates how gentle, exploratory movement can bypass restrictions. Through small, careful movements with attention to sensation, the nervous system can learn new options. This approach shows that when we reduce effort and explore with curiosity, our organization can improve naturally, without forcing or straining.

Creating Conditions for Expression

This stage invites a particular kind of attention - neither making movement happen nor waiting passively, but creating conditions where movement can emerge naturally. Sometimes this manifests as subtle shifts - a finger wanting to stretch, shoulders wanting to soften, the spine wanting to undulate. Other times it might develop into fuller expression - rolling, reaching, rising to walk or dance.

The key is maintaining connection with the authentic impulse rather than falling into habitual patterns or performance. This allows movement to emerge as a natural expression of our internal state, supporting integration between sensation, emotion, and action. Over time, this practice can help us rediscover movement possibilities that were always available but had become inaccessible through habit or protection.

From Movement to Held Awareness

As we become more familiar with following movement impulses, we begin to notice something interesting: there's a space between feeling an impulse and acting on it. This subtle gap offers an opportunity for a different kind of exploration. Rather than immediately expressing through movement, we can hold the impulse in awareness and let it reveal more of its nature.

This shift from immediate expression to held awareness represents what Lecours and Bouchard identify as a crucial development in how we process experience. The capacity to contain and explore impulses, rather than simply discharging them through action, opens up new possibilities for meaning and integration. Like a chrysalis creating the conditions for transformation, this contained awareness allows natural processes of reorganization to occur.

The Chrysalis Stage: Creating Space for Transformation

The Chrysalis stage cultivates the capacity to hold experience in awareness without immediately acting. In Lecours and Bouchard's framework, this represents a crucial shift in how we process experience - from immediate discharge to increasingly sophisticated forms of containment. Their research shows how this ability to hold and modulate experience creates conditions where new forms of understanding can emerge.

The Neuroscience of Pause

Neural Networks and Integration

The brain organizes experience through distinct but interconnected networks. The default mode network activates during internal reflection, supporting meaning-making and self-reference. Meanwhile, the salience network helps determine what needs attention and supports choosing appropriate responses. When we create space between impulse and action, these networks can engage more fully, enabling more flexible and integrated responses.

Memory and Learning

Research on memory and learning provides crucial insights into how this pause supports transformation. When memories or patterns become active, they temporarily enter a state where they can be updated. During this window, holding experience in awareness while feeling safe allows new associations to form. This process, known as memory reconsolidation, helps explain how sustained attention in the right conditions can transform old patterns.

Studies of meditation and mindfulness reveal how sustained attention affects brain function. Regular practice increases gray matter in areas involved in emotional regulation and strengthens connections between emotion and reasoning centers. This integration supports more balanced responses and better access to different kinds of knowing. Importantly, this integration happens not through force but through the gentle holding of awareness.

Psychological Understanding

Winnicott's Potential Space

D.W. Winnicott's concept of potential space illuminates how transformation occurs in this stage. He describes a special state of consciousness that emerges when we feel safe enough to explore but not constrained - neither purely subjective nor objective, but a "third area" where play and creativity naturally arise. In this space, we can engage with experience more freely, allowing new meanings and possibilities to emerge.

The Window of Tolerance

This understanding aligns with trauma therapy's concept of the window of tolerance - the zone where we can stay present with experience while maintaining integration. Outside this window, we either become overwhelmed or shut down, losing access to our full capabilities. The Chrysalis stage works to expand this window gradually, creating conditions where we can hold increasingly complex experience while staying regulated.

Traditional Wisdom

Buddhist Insight

The Buddhist tradition offers detailed maps of how awareness transforms experience. Through sustained attention, practitioners observe how experience constantly changes and how fixed ideas about self become more fluid. This reveals how much of what we take as solid reality is actually construction, and how presence itself can allow natural wisdom to emerge.

Taoist Wu-Wei

The Taoist concept of wu-wei (non-doing) provides insight into effortless action. Rather than forcing change, it emphasizes creating conditions where transformation can happen naturally. This might seem paradoxical - how can not-doing create change? The answer lies in understanding how change often happens best through alignment with natural process rather than through force.

Movement Arts

Various movement traditions work with this delicate balance between stillness and action. The practice of "sung" in Tai Chi involves releasing unnecessary tension while maintaining alert presence. Zen arts like calligraphy cultivate the ability to move from collected stillness, expressing directly without interference. These practices demonstrate how contained awareness can support more integrated and authentic expression.

Creating Conditions for Transformation

In practice, this stage involves a particular quality of attention to movement impulses. Rather than either acting on impulses or suppressing them, we explore what it's like to experience them fully. This creates space to notice their qualities - their intensity, their texture, how they change and move within us.

This contained awareness allows us to engage imagination more fully. We can explore what an impulse might do if anything were possible, letting our mind play freely with possibilities. Like a child at play, we enter a space of pure creativity where anything is possible. This playful engagement often reveals deeper meanings and connections we might have missed if we moved immediately into action.

The practice shows how transformation often happens not through doing but through creating the right conditions. By holding experience with spacious awareness, we allow natural processes of integration and reorganization to occur. This supports a kind of change that feels organic and authentic rather than forced or imposed.

From Held Awareness to Witnessed Expression

The capacity to hold experience in awareness, developed in the Chrysalis stage, creates the foundation for a particular kind of sharing. When we can stay present with our experience without immediately acting or speaking, we discover deeper layers of meaning that want to be expressed. This represents the transition in Lecours and Bouchard's framework from held awareness to verbal expression - not just putting words to experience, but allowing new forms of understanding to emerge through witnessed sharing.

This stage builds upon all the previous ones: the somatic awareness that keeps us grounded in direct experience, the motoric understanding that helps us sense the living quality of what we're sharing, and the contained awareness that allows us to find words that truly resonate with our experience rather than falling into habitual patterns of expression.

The Butterfly Stage: Integration Through Witnessed Expression

The final stage of the practice creates a container for shared meaning-making through witnessed expression. In Lecours and Bouchard's model of mentalization, the verbal register represents the most sophisticated level where experience can be symbolized and shared. However, this stage isn't simply about putting words to experience - it involves a fundamental transformation in how experience is held and understood through the unique qualities of witnessed presence.

The Art of Deep Listening

At the heart of this stage lies what psychodynamic theory calls reverie - a special quality of mental space that emerges through deep listening. In reverie, we attend not just to words, but to tone, gesture, and our own internal responses while allowing space for silence. This process engages what neuroscience identifies as right-brain communication. Beneath our conscious verbal exchanges, our emotional and embodied systems engage in constant subtle dialogue. When both people attend to their internal experience while staying in relationship, a kind of resonance can emerge, like two tuning forks beginning to vibrate in harmony. This creates moments of being understood at levels deeper than words can easily reach.

The neuroscience of human connection illuminates how this works. When we're in attuned presence with another person, our nervous systems begin to synchronize, activating right-hemisphere brain regions involved in emotional processing and embodied understanding. This synchronization supports deep emotional regulation and integration, creating conditions where different aspects of experience - sensory, emotional, and cognitive - can come together in new ways.

Creating Containers for Transformation

The Power of Witnessing

This quality of presence, combined with spaciousness and attention to subtle internal signals, creates containers where new kinds of understanding become possible. Being truly seen and accepted beneath our usual social interactions can transform how we relate to our own experience. The presence of an attuned witness helps create conditions where integration can happen naturally, allowing experience to be not just expressed but truly understood in new ways.

Traditional and Clinical Wisdom

The power of this witnessing relationship has been validated by both psychotherapy research and traditional wisdom. Various traditions, from indigenous council practices to contemplative traditions, have long recognized how this kind of witnessed speaking supports transformation. They understand that something profound happens in the space between speaking and listening when it's held with care and presence.

The Dance of Speaking and Listening

When we speak in this container, we're invited to attend to our internal experience and find words that feel true to it. This isn't about performing or explaining, but about discovering what wants to be expressed. When we listen, we're invited into a state of receptive presence - suspending our usual habits of planning responses or trying to fix or guide the sharing. Instead, we let ourselves be touched by the subtle shifts in our partner's presence and notice how their sharing resonates in our own being.

Through this dance of speaking and listening, new meanings emerge, different aspects of experience integrate, and natural healing movements complete. The result is often a greater coherence in experience, more flexibility in response, and enhanced capacity for authentic connection. Most importantly, it develops our ability to hold and understand experience in new ways, supporting both deeper self-understanding and more genuine connection with others.

Integration: The Butterfly Transformation

Natural Progression

The practice's progression through these stages reflects a deep understanding of how human experience naturally transforms. Each stage builds essential capacities that support the ones that follow:

  • The Egg stage develops our ability to stay with direct experience rather than immediately moving into thinking or doing. This somatic foundation supports all later stages.
  • The Caterpillar stage helps us discover how movement can emerge naturally from awareness, teaching us to trust and follow our authentic impulses rather than forcing or performing.
  • The Chrysalis stage builds our capacity to hold experience in awareness, allowing natural processes of integration to occur rather than rushing to express or resolve.
  • The Butterfly stage brings all these capacities together - somatic awareness, authentic movement, and held attention - in the container of witnessed expression, supporting deeper integration and understanding.

Research Support

This progression aligns with both ancient wisdom and modern research about how transformation happens. Traditional practices across cultures have discovered similar patterns of development, while contemporary neuroscience and psychology help us understand the mechanisms involved.

Most importantly, the practice honors how change actually occurs in living systems. Just as a butterfly emerges through natural processes when conditions support it, our capacity for integrated expression emerges naturally when we create the right conditions. We don't need to force transformation - we need only support the conditions where it can unfold naturally.

A Note on Safety

Because this practice works with deep layers of experience, careful attention to safety is essential. The container is structured to support safe exploration through:

  • Progressive development of capacities
  • Emphasis on internal listening and self-direction
  • Clear containment in paired sharing
  • Focus on present experience
  • Following natural impulses rather than forcing

Participants are encouraged to trust their own timing, modify activities as needed, take breaks when helpful, set comfortable boundaries, and stay within their window of tolerance.
