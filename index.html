The Natural Tension

Natural Instincts

We're born with instincts for movement, expression, and connection. These natural impulses draw us toward growth and authenticity, showing us who we could be.

Learned Protection

In a world with real consequences, we learn to protect ourselves. Fear of judgement, hurt, or isolation teaches us to constrict - to make ourselves smaller in both body and feeling.

Understanding Restriction

When fear leads us to contain our natural expression, its influence reaches deep:

In Our Body

We learn to hold back physical impulses through tension and restricted movement. What begins as protection becomes a habit of holding ourselves in check, gradually diminishing our natural capacity for movement and expression.

In Our Emotions

Our mind develops ways to shield us from feelings that seem unsafe - it may tell us stories to avoid vulnerability, uncertainty, or being overwhelmed. It shapes how we see reality to keep our emotional world smaller, limiting our ability to fully experience and process our feelings.

In Our Relationships

The ways we protect ourselves shape our relationships in ways we might not even see, sometimes recreating the very patterns we were trying to avoid.

Finding Freedom

My exploration centers on these parallel paths. Throughout history and across cultures - from contemporary movement culture to yoga, martial arts, traditional dance forms, and meditative movement practices like qigong and Sufi whirling - people have discovered how mindful movement both expands physical freedom and helps rediscover natural playful expression. Similarly, but through a different path, depth-oriented understanding offers its own journey of transformation, using consistent internal attention to gradually reshape our relationship with ourselves and create space for authentic expression.

Movement & Embodiment

  • Developing physical strength, mobility, and skill to support confident movement
  • Building awareness of internal sensations and natural impulses
  • Rediscovering joy and playfulness in expression
  • Creating a bridge back to parts we've kept small

Therapeutic Depth

  • Exploring and reshaping our internal world through reflection and connection
  • Learning new ways to hold our experience
  • Building emotional capacity as we meet what we've been avoiding
  • Freeing up energy previously used for protection

These approaches work together to create space for both safety and growth. As our inner world expands, we discover a natural freedom to move, feel, and connect in ways that express our authentic selves.

Movement & Emotion: The Butterfly Transformation

Drawing from Lecours & Bouchard's theory of emotional processing and its different "registers" of experience, I've been exploring how the metaphor of butterfly transformation might help us understand the journey through these registers. In their model, emotional experience can be processed at somatic, motoric, imaginal, and verbal levels. I've found that mapping these registers onto the stages of butterfly transformation offers an accessible way to explore how we can move through these different ways of processing and transforming experience.

The Butterfly Framework

Building on the concept of emotional registers, this metaphor explores different ways of holding experience:

  • Egg (Somatic Register): The raw sensations of experience in the body - creating a safe container to notice what's present
  • Caterpillar (Motoric Register): Natural movement impulses - allowing organic expression while maintaining groundedness
  • Chrysalis (Imaginal Register): The emergence of images and metaphors - like dreams and artistic expression naturally do
  • Butterfly (Verbal Register): Finding words if they arise - no pressure to explain or analyse

Integration Points

  • Contemporary psychological understanding of how we process experience
  • Movement traditions' recognition of the body's natural wisdom
  • Contemplative practices' ways of holding experience with awareness
  • Creative expression as a natural way of processing experience

Movement Practice

Different people naturally resonate with different aspects of this practice. Some might find pure movement most accessible, others might naturally move toward imagery or verbal expression. This isn't about progressing through rigid stages, but rather about exploring different ways experience can be held and transformed.

  • Move at your own pace and depth
  • Engage with the aspects that resonate for you
  • No pressure to achieve any particular state or insight
  • Freedom to explore your own balance of structure and flow
